Hummingbird Spatial & Temporal Acuity

Hummingbird perched inside the Temporal Acuity Chamber

Hummingbird inside the Spatial Acuity Chamber

The sampling of spatial and temporal visual information for all living organisms is finite. The accuracy and speed of visual systems contributes in part to an animal's sensitivity to visual motion. The ability to see swift motions is a crucial adaptation among bird species, which are high-speed animals that navigate in a three-dimensional world. Until recently, our understanding in how animals utilise their vision in flight have been limited to invertebrates models only. Hummingbirds have become the ideal model species to bridge our understanding of visual guidance between invertebrates and vertebrates as they are vertebrates that has the unique capacity to sustain hover, which is a hallmark feature of most volant invertebrates.

At the time of my Master's of Science degree with the Altshuler lab, hummingbirds' sensitivity to visual information, both spatially and temporally, remained unknown. Using operant conditioning and optocollic reflex experiments, I identified the temporal and spatial acuity thresholds of the Anna's hummingbird's visual system for my MSc thesis. These data along with histological examination of hummingbird retinas were published in Physiological and Biochemical Zoology, marking the first time both spatial and temporal acuity was measured in a sustained hovering animal.

Visual Guidance in Hummingbirds

Birds can steer a precise course at high speed, but little is known about how they avoid collisions with surrounding objects and the ground. We manipulated the visual environment of hummingbirds as they flew across a long chamber to evaluate how they use visual information for course control. Our results suggest that in natural settings, birds may avoid collisions by monitoring the vertical size, expansion, and relative position of obstacles.
